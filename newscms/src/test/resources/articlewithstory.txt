</figure><p class="story-body__introduction">At least 832 people were killed in the devastating earthquake and tsunami that hit the Indonesian island of Sulawesi, the national disaster agency says.</p><p>Many people were reported trapped in the rubble of buildings that collapsed in Friday's 7.5-magnitude earthquake, agency spokesman Sutopo Purwo Nugroho told a news conference.</p><p>The quake triggered tsunami waves as high as 6m (20ft), he added.</p><p>Vice-President Jusuf Kalla said the final death toll could be thousands.</p><p>Rescuers have been digging by hand in the search for survivors in the city of Palu.</p><div id="bbccom_mpu_1_2_3" class="bbccom_slot mpu-ad" aria-hidden="true">

I have my staff on the ground, but it's impossible just to rely on their strength alone to clear this," Muhammad Syaugi, head of the national search-and-rescue agency, told AFP news agency.</p><p>There have also been concerns about the town of Donggala, where the impact is still unclear.</p><ul class="story-body__unordered-list"><li class="story-body__list-item"><a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-asia-45697233" class="story-body__link">In pictures: Search for survivors</a></li></ul><p>The Red Cross estimates that more than 1.6 million people have been affected by the earthquake and tsunami which it described as a tragedy that "could get much worse".</p><figure class="media-portrait no-caption full-width">

        </figure><p>Strong aftershocks have continued to hit the island since Friday's earthquake.</p><p>President Joko Widodo visited Palu to view sites affected by the disaster, including Talise Beach - the main tourist area that was badly affected by the tsunami.</p><p>"I know there are many problems that need to be solved in a short time, including communications," he said. </p><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">What is the situation in Palu?</h2><p>Many remain missing in the city of 335,000, some thought to be trapped in the debris of collapsed buildings.</p><p>Rescue teams dug by hand to free 24 people trapped in the rubble of the Roa-Roa hotel in the city of Palu, but there are fears that dozens more remain trapped. </p><figure class="media-landscape has-caption full-width">

Clean water is scarce. The minimarkets are looted everywhere."</p><hr class="story-body__line"><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">'Supplies running out'</h2><p><strong>By Rebecca Henschke, in Poso</strong></p><p>In Poso, a four-hour drive from Palu, supplies are running out. State-owned petrol stations have closed as they have run dry. People are queuing up at roadside stalls, buying petrol in bottles to take into the affected area and to find missing loved ones. Supermarkets have limited food left and it is difficult to find bottled water. </p><p>Ermi Liana, who is travelling with our BBC team, doesn't know if her parents are alive. "They live close to a bridge that collapsed. I can't reach them by phone," she says. "I can only pray they are alive."</p><p>We haven't seen any aid heading in and there is still no communication link to the town of Donggala.</p><hr class="story-body__line"><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">Why were so many killed on Friday?</h2><p><a href="https://www.usgs.gov/news/magnitude-75-earthquake-near-palu-indonesia" class="story-body__link-external">The 7.5 magnitude quake occurred at a shallow depth of 10km (6.2 miles)</a> just off the central island of Sulawesi at 18:03 (10:03 GMT), triggering a tsunami, US monitors say. </p><p>Many people were on the beach in Palu, preparing for a festival, and were caught when waves swept in.</p><p>Video shows people screaming and fleeing in panic. </p><figure class="media-portrait no-caption full-width">

    </div>    <figcaption class="media-with-caption__caption"><span class="off-screen">Media caption</span>The moment the tsunami hit</figcaption>
</figure><p>When the quake hit, "we all panicked and ran out of the house," Anser Bachmid, 39, told AFP news agency. "People here need aid - food, drink, clean water. We don't know what to eat for dinner tonight." </p><p>"I just ran when I saw the waves hitting homes on the coastline," Palu resident Rusidanto said.</p><p>Dwi Haris, who was in the city for a wedding, was staying in a hotel with his wife and daughter when the quake struck.</p><p><a href="https://apnews.com/d056b4d022754cbd989030242f93202d?utm_source=Twitter&amp;utm_campaign=SocialFlow&amp;utm_medium=AP" class="story-body__link-external">"There was no time to save ourselves,"</a> he told the Associated Press news agency. "I was squeezed into the ruins of the wall... I heard my wife cry for help but then silence. I don't know what happened to her and my child. I hope they are safe."</p><p>With back and shoulder injuries, he is being treated outdoors at Palu's Army Hospital.</p><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">What is being done to help?</h2><p>Aid is being flown from the capital Jakarta into Palu airport, using the part of its runway still intact.</p><p>Patients are being treated in the open outside city hospitals and at least one military field hospital has been erected.</p><p>The regional head of the Indonesian Doctors Association (IDI), Komang, asked the authorities for immediate help. </p><p>"We need tents, medicines, medical personnel, tarpaulins, blankets and more of other things," he said.</p><p>The UK-based charity Save the Children is sending an assessment team to the disaster zone.</p><p>"Unfortunately the more information that we're getting, the worse the situation appears to be," charity spokesman Tom Howells told the BBC from Jakarta. </p><hr class="story-body__line"><p><strong>Have you been affected by the earthquake?
